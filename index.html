Layer 01 — 0–35 km

The crust

Thin, cool, and brittle — the only part of the planet we've ever touched directly. Under the oceans it's barely 5 km thick; under the continents, up to 70. Every mountain, root, and fossil you'll ever see lives inside this shell.

Layer 02 — 35–660 km

Upper mantle

Below the crust, rock softens into rock that flows — slowly, like tar. This is the asthenosphere, the layer that lets tectonic plates glide and collide over millions of years, building the mountains layer one folds into layer two.

Layer 03 — 660–2,900 km

Lower mantle

Pressure here reaches 1.4 million atmospheres. Minerals are crushed into denser, unfamiliar crystal forms — bridgmanite, the most abundant mineral on Earth, exists almost nowhere but here, and nowhere on the surface at all.

Layer 04 — 2,900–5,150 km

Outer core

Solid rock gives way to liquid metal — a churning ocean of molten iron and nickel. Its convection currents generate the electric currents that generate Earth's magnetic field, the invisible shield that keeps the solar wind from stripping our atmosphere away.

Layer 05 — 5,150–6,371 km

Inner core

Hot enough to melt iron twice over, yet solid — squeezed into a crystal lattice by pressure alone. It's roughly the size of the Moon, growing by about a millimeter a year as the planet slowly cools, and it's almost as old as Earth itself.

You've reached the center.
